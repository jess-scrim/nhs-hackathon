1. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

This report assesses which time series forecasting methodology is most appropriate to support analysis of paediatric service data within Welsh Government. It is a methodology selection report; empirical findings will be presented in a separate, subsequent report once a methodology has been confirmed and analysis has been completed.


The key question this work seeks to answer is: given the nature of paediatric demand and activity data available to the modelling team, which forecasting approach will produce the most reliable, interpretable, and policy-relevant projections?


[Why This Matters]
  Paediatric services face significant demand pressures. Accurate forecasting is essential to inform workforce planning, capacity decisions, and commissioning. Selecting the wrong methodology risks producing projections that mislead rather than guide decision-making.


What We Know So Far
───────────────────

A range of established time series forecasting methods have been reviewed. Each method involves trade-offs between accuracy, interpretability, and the volume and quality of data required. The right choice depends heavily on the characteristics of the available paediatric data.

2. BACKGROUND AND PURPOSE
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Welsh Government's modelling team has been tasked with identifying which time series forecasting methodology is best suited to answer questions arising from paediatric service analysis.


Paediatric services — covering hospital activity, outpatient demand, waiting times, and related indicators — generate data with specific characteristics: seasonal patterns (e.g. winter pressures, school-year cycles), potential structural breaks (e.g. COVID-19), and relatively small sample sizes compared with adult services. These features make methodology selection non-trivial.

3. METHODOLOGY SUMMARY (TECHNICAL)

────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Candidate Methodologies
───────────────────────

The following methods have been assessed against the criteria of: (a) suitability for paediatric demand data characteristics; (b) interpretability for policy audiences; (c) performance with smaller, potentially irregular datasets; (d) computational feasibility within team capability.


Methodology | Data Required | Computational Cost | Interpretability | Key Constraint
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
ARIMA / SARIMA | Moderate | Low–Moderate | High | Assumes stationarity; limited with small samples
Exponential Smoothing | Low | Low | High | Best for smooth, short-horizon trends
Prophet (Facebook) | Low | Moderate | High | Handles seasonality and holidays well
LSTM / Deep Learning | High | High | Low | Requires large, clean datasets
Ensemble Methods | High | High | Moderate | Highest accuracy but least interpretable


Selection Criteria
──────────────────

  Data Volume and Quality

Organisational data may have gaps, reclassifications, or inconsistent recording periods. Methods that are robust to missing data and sensitive to structural breaks (e.g. Prophet, which explicitly models changepoints, or SARIMA with intervention terms) are preferred over methods that assume clean, continuous series.


  Forecast Horizon

[INFORMATION NEEDED: The appropriate methodology varies materially by forecast horizon. Short-horizon forecasting favours simpler exponential smoothing or ARIMA approaches; longer horizons may require structural models or ensemble methods. Please confirm the required horizon.]


  Interpretability Requirements

Welsh Government policy outputs require transparent, auditable modelling. Methods where parameter estimates have clear interpretations (ARIMA, exponential smoothing, Prophet) are strongly preferred over neural network or ensemble approaches unless accuracy gains are substantial and the additional complexity can be justified and communicated.


  Seasonality

Paediatric data is likely to exhibit strong annual and possibly term-time seasonality. SARIMA (Seasonal ARIMA) and Prophet both handle multiple seasonalities natively. Simple exponential smoothing (Holt-Winters) is appropriate for single seasonality. LSTM networks can capture complex seasonal patterns but require significantly more data.


Validation Approach
───────────────────

Whichever methodology is selected, the following validation steps are recommended:
  • Time series cross-validation (walk-forward validation) rather than random train/test splits, to respect temporal ordering.
  • Error metrics reported as both MAPE (Mean Absolute Percentage Error) and RMSE to capture both relative and absolute performance.
  • Residual diagnostics (Ljung-Box test for autocorrelation, Shapiro-Wilk for normality of residuals).
  • Sensitivity analysis on key parameters (e.g. differencing order for ARIMA, changepoint prior for Prophet).

5. LIMITATIONS AND CAVEATS (TECHNICAL)
════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

[⚠  Non-Official Data — Primary Limitation]
  All analysis is based on organisational data. This is the primary methodological constraint. Until a data quality assessment is complete, the following risks are unquantified: definitional drift over time; inconsistent reporting periods; incomplete or suppressed records; linkage errors if data is derived from multiple administrative systems.


Specific Technical Limitations
──────────────────────────────
  • Sample size: Paediatric series are typically smaller than adult equivalents. Methods requiring large training datasets (LSTM, gradient boosting ensembles) may underperform or overfit.
  • Structural breaks: COVID-19 and any associated service changes represent potential structural breaks. Failure to account for these will invalidate stationarity assumptions in ARIMA-family models. Formal break testing (e.g. Chow test, Bai-Perron) is recommended prior to model fitting.
  • Forecast uncertainty: Prediction intervals must be reported alongside point forecasts. For organisational data, intervals should be widened to reflect additional uncertainty from data quality issues.
  • Extrapolation risk: All time series forecasts are extrapolations. Results become less reliable as the forecast horizon increases. Scenarios should supplement point forecasts.
  • Analytical question underspecification: The precise dependent variable, forecast horizon, and policy use case have not been confirmed. Methodology selection is therefore provisional. [INFORMATION NEEDED: See Section 2.]
  • Model selection uncertainty: Without empirical results, it is not possible to confirm which methodology performs best on this specific dataset. The selection framework in Section 3 provides a structured basis for that decision once data is available.

B. Glossary
───────────
  • ARIMA: Autoregressive Integrated Moving Average — a classical statistical forecasting model for univariate time series.
  • SARIMA: Seasonal ARIMA — extends ARIMA to handle periodic seasonal patterns.
  • Prophet: An open-source forecasting tool developed by Meta, designed for business time series with strong seasonality and irregular data.
  • LSTM: Long Short-Term Memory — a class of recurrent neural network suited to sequential data with long-range dependencies.
  • MAPE: Mean Absolute Percentage Error — a common forecast accuracy metric expressed as a percentage of actual values.
  • RMSE: Root Mean Squared Error — a measure of forecast error that penalises large deviations more heavily than MAPE.
  • Structural break: A point in a time series where the underlying data-generating process changes, e.g. due to a policy change or pandemic.
  • Walk-forward validation: A time series cross-validation technique that simulates real forecasting by training on past data and testing on future data sequentially.
